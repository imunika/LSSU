About


The Landscape Studies at Syracuse University website emerged in the midst of the COVID-19 pandemic. It is testament to the camaraderie of the faculty who shaped the intellectual life of the seminar, as well as to the collegiality of the graduate students who were invited to contribute to the website at an extraordinary moment in human history. At a time when we are more acutely aware than ever about the limits of spatiality imposed by social distancing upon the human body, and the spaces those very same limits have opened up for nonhuman animals, the very conceptualization of landscapes as bounded spaces, cultural constructions, and social imaginaries has been fundamentally challenged. Landscapes, we have learned, are anything but fixed. They are fluid, impermanent, and discontinuous even as they overlap and intersect with one another. They are permeable yet subject to limits and codes. And they are rooted in the dynamics of movement and corporeality. Masking has inscribed new boundaries between the outdoors and the indoors, and between and within different bodies, radically altering our understanding of interiority, space, and corporeality. Landscape Studies at Syracuse University gave its participants, the agents of its landscaping, the opportunity to reflect on these topical issues, as well as on their ongoing research and creative projects. It also features select guest lectures by eminent speakers who brought their fresh perspectives on people, plants, animals, and cityscapes to the seminar. Funded by the CUSE Grant Program at Syracuse University, the website is curated by Romita Ray in the department of Art and Music Histories in the College of Arts and Sciences, who is deeply grateful to her colleagues and students for taking the time to share their research and reflections. She thanks guest speakers Bettina Stoetzer (MIT), Harriet Ritvo (MIT), and Mickey Mahan (Syracuse) for enhancing the seminar discussions and exchange. And she is indebted to art history major Bridget Claire Fullam (Syracuse University) for transcribing the YouTube videos featured here. The website was designed by Elizabeth Novoa (Unika Analytics) in collaboration with Daniel Acuna (School of Information Studies) at Syracuse University.
